The Method

The basic indicators in this study come from an algorithm, that analyzes the persons you follow on Twitter who form the largest part of your newsfeed (apart from advertisements included by Twitter itself). The script has a list of Twitter users (mostly politicians and news outlets) whose political ideology has been confirmed. Then, simply put, the algorithm counts the number of persons you follow in either direction (liberal or conservative) and calculates a score representing the balance of your follower list.

×

In a second step, the method is extended by persons who are predominantly followed by left- or right-leaning users and are therefore also relatively safe to incorporate in the analysis (in addition to politicians and news outlets).

More specifically, the algorithm supposes that every user has a probability to follow another Twitter user. This probability is influenced by the level of the political interest of the following user and the ideological distance between the user chosing to follow and the user being followed. The algorithm also accounts for the popularity of users: e.g. politicians such as @BarackObama or @realDonaldTrump have a higher chance of being followed by a random user than regular senators.

References

The calculations and the script are taken from Prof. Dr. Pablo Barberá . You can find and use his scripts with the program R by installing the tweetscores package. Please also have a look at the scientific publications using this script:

  • Barberá, P. (2015). Birds of the Same Feather Tweet Together: Bayesian Ideal Point Estimation Using Twitter Data. Political Analysis, 23(1), 76-91. DOI
  • Barberá, P., John Jost, Jonathan Nagler, Joshua Tucker and Richard Bonneau (2015). Tweeting from Left to Right: Is Online Political Communication More Than an Echo Chamber?. Psychological Science, 26(10), 1531-1542. DOI

The survey was built with Unipark and the results-website was created with a Shiny framework, published via a Shiny-server which was hosted on a virtual machine of the University of Konstanz. This website is programmed in Python Flask.
